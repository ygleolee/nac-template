  \kactlimport{LinearSieve.h}
  Mobius identity: $\sum_{d|n} \mu(d) = I[n = 1]$. Example usage:
  \(
  \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} I[\text{gcd}(i, j) = 1]
  = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \sum_{d|gcd(i, j)} \mu(d)
  = \sum_{d=1}^{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} I[d | i] \sum_{j=1}^{n} I[d | j] \mu(d)
  = \sum_{d=1}^{n} \lfloor i/d \rfloor \lfloor j/d \rfloor \mu(d)
  \)
  Multiplicative functions: $f(pq) = f(p)f(q)$ if $\text{gcd}(p, q) = 1$, can calculate using linear sieve. When we don't know the closed form for $p | q$, let $\text{cnt}(q)$ denote the power of the smallest prime (in this case $p$ by the linear sieve) that divides $q$, we have
  $f(p \cdot q) = f \left( p^{\text{cnt}(q) + 1} \right) \cdot f(q) \cdot f \left( p^{\text{cnt}(q)} \right)^{-1}$. $\text{cnt}$ can be calculated along with the linear sieve (see example).

	\subsection{Bézout's identity}
	For $a \neq $, $b \neq 0$, then $d=gcd(a,b)$ is the smallest positive integer for which there are integer solutions to
	$$ax+by=d$$
	If $(x,y)$ is one solution, then all solutions are given by
	$$\left(x+\frac{kb}{\gcd(a,b)}, y-\frac{ka}{\gcd(a,b)}\right), \quad k\in\mathbb{Z}$$

\section{Pythagorean Triples}
 The Pythagorean triples are uniquely generated by
 \[ a=k\cdot (m^{2}-n^{2}),\ \,b=k\cdot (2mn),\ \,c=k\cdot (m^{2}+n^{2}), \]
 with $m > n > 0$, $k > 0$, $m \bot n$, and either $m$ or $n$ even.
